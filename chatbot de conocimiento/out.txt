Abstract

Large language models have rapidly evolved from static text generators into interactive
conversational systems. However, most deployed systems remain fundamentally shorthorizon
and answer-centric, limiting their applicability in complex, knowledge-intensive tasks that
require sustained reasoning, memory, and reliability over time. As interest grows in agentic
generative AI, there is a pressing need for architectural frameworks that move beyond chat-
based interaction toward systems capable of long-horizon knowledge acquisition and decision
support. This paper introduces the concept of knowledge agents-evidence-aware generative
systems designed to operate over extended temporal and cognitive horizons. We propose an
architectural framework that integrates retrieval-augmented generation, persistent memory,
iterative reasoning, and explicit evidence evaluation into a uniﬁed agent loop. Unlike traditional
chatbots, knowledge agents continuously assess the adequacy and stability of their supporting
evidence, adapt retrieval strategies based on knowledge gaps, and surface uncertainty when
information is insuﬃcient. We analyze key architectural components required for such systems,
including episodic and semantic memory, evidence-aware retrieval controllers, and self-
evaluation mechanisms that monitor grounding quality across reasoning steps. The framework
emphasizes transparency, traceability, and epistemic reliability rather than response ﬂuency.
We present experimental results from a working implementation using local models (Ollama's
Llama 3) and vector stores (ChromaDB), demonstrating the framework's eﬀectiveness with
real experiments showing average conﬁdence scores of 0.78, evidence coverage of 0.63, and
100% success rate across test queries. Through illustrative workﬂows and design patterns, we
demonstrate how knowledge agents can support long-horizon tasks such as policy analysis,
scientiﬁc exploration, and strategic planning. This work provides a conceptual foundation
for next-generation generative AI systems that prioritize sustained reasoning and trustworthy
knowledge use. By formalizing the transition from chatbots to knowledge agents, the paper

1     Introduction
The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has transformed the landscape of
artificial intelligence, enabling systems that can engage in natural language conversation, an-
swer questions, and generate coherent text across diverse domains. However, despite these
remarkable capabilities, most deployed generative AI systems remain constrained by fundamen-
tal architectural limitations that prevent them from operating effectively in knowledge-intensive,
long-horizon scenarios.

1.1    The Current State of Generative AI
Contemporary generative AI systems, particularly those based on transformer architectures [1],
excel at producing fluent, contextually appropriate responses within short conversational turns.
These systems have demonstrated impressive performance on benchmarks measuring factual
recall, reasoning ability, and language understanding. Yet, when deployed in real-world scenarios
that require sustained knowledge acquisition, multi-step reasoning, and evidence-based decision-
making, they reveal significant shortcomings.
    Most current systems are stateless—each interaction is treated as an isolated event, with no
persistent memory of past interactions or learned knowledge. They are short-horizon—optimized
for immediate responses rather than extended reasoning chains or long-term planning. Perhaps
most critically, they are answer-focused —designed to produce a response quickly rather than
to engage in the iterative process of knowledge building, evidence gathering, and confidence
assessment that characterizes human expertise.

1.2    The Need for Knowledge Agents
As generative AI systems are increasingly called upon to support complex tasks such as policy
analysis, scientific research, strategic planning, and long-term decision support, the limitations
of chat-based architectures become apparent. These domains require:

    • Long-term memory: The ability to accumulate and reference knowledge across multiple
      sessions and time periods

    • Multi-step reasoning: The capacity to break down complex problems into sequences of
      reasoning steps, each building on previous conclusions

    • Evidence tracking: Explicit tracking of what information supports each claim or deci-
      sion, and how that evidence evolves over time

    • Reliability assessment: Self-evaluation mechanisms that can identify when information
      is insufficient, contradictory, or uncertain

   These requirements point toward a new class of systems that we term knowledge agents—generative
AI architectures designed specifically for evidence-aware, long-horizon knowledge work.

1.3    Contributions
This paper makes the following contributions:

    1. We formalize the concept of Knowledge Agents as a distinct system class, distinguish-
       ing them from traditional chatbots through explicit architectural requirements for memory,
       evidence tracking, and long-horizon reasoning.

2     Limitations of Chat-Based Systems
To understand why knowledge agents represent a necessary evolution, we must first examine
the fundamental limitations of current chat-based generative AI systems. While these systems
have achieved remarkable success in many domains, they exhibit three critical weaknesses when
applied to knowledge-intensive, long-horizon tasks.

2.1    Statelessness
Most deployed generative AI systems are fundamentally stateless. Each conversation turn is
processed independently, with context limited to the current session’s conversation history. This
design choice, while simplifying implementation and enabling stateless scaling, creates several
problems:

    • No persistent learning: Systems cannot accumulate knowledge across sessions. A user’s
      previous interactions, insights, or corrections are lost once the session ends.
    • Repeated work: Users must re-establish context, re-explain requirements, and re-teach
      the system in each new session.
    • No knowledge evolution: The system cannot track how its understanding of a topic
      has changed over time, or maintain a coherent knowledge base that grows with use.

2.2    Short-Horizon Reasoning
Current systems are optimized for immediate response generation rather than extended reasoning
chains. This short-horizon focus manifests in several ways:

    • Context window limitations: While context windows have grown significantly, they
      remain finite and often insufficient for complex, multi-step reasoning tasks that require
      referencing large amounts of information.

   • No explicit planning: Systems do not typically engage in explicit planning or goal
     decomposition. They generate responses reactively rather than strategically.
   • Limited iteration: The typical interaction pattern is a single query followed by a single
     response, with limited mechanisms for iterative refinement or multi-turn reasoning.

2.3   Answer-Centric Design
Perhaps the most fundamental limitation is the answer-centric nature of current systems. They
are designed to produce a response quickly, rather than to engage in the process of knowledge
building:

   • No evidence tracking: Systems do not explicitly track what information supports each
     claim, making it difficult to assess reliability or trace conclusions back to sources.
   • No uncertainty quantification: While some systems can express uncertainty, they lack
     systematic mechanisms for assessing when information is insufficient or contradictory.
   • No self-evaluation: Systems rarely evaluate the adequacy of their own responses or
     identify knowledge gaps that should be filled before responding.
   • Fluency over reliability: The training objective prioritizes fluent, coherent text gener-
     ation over epistemic reliability or evidence grounding.

2.4   Consequences for Knowledge-Intensive Tasks
These limitations have significant consequences when systems are applied to knowledge-intensive
tasks:
Example 2.1 (Policy Analysis). Consider a system tasked with analyzing a proposed policy
change. A chat-based system might provide an immediate response based on its training data,
but it cannot:
   • Track how the policy landscape has evolved over time
   • Maintain a knowledge base of related policies and their outcomes
   • Assess whether it has sufficient information to make a reliable recommendation
   • Provide traceable evidence for each claim
Example 2.2 (Scientific Exploration). In scientific research, a knowledge agent should be able
to:
   • Accumulate knowledge about a research question across multiple sessions
   • Track conflicting evidence and identify areas of uncertainty
   • Build upon previous reasoning steps rather than starting from scratch each time
   • Explicitly link conclusions to supporting evidence
A chat-based system cannot support these requirements effectively.

2.5   The Path Forward
These limitations are not inherent to generative AI technology itself, but rather to the architec-
tural choices made in current systems. By explicitly designing for long-horizon, evidence-aware
operation, we can create systems that address these limitations while retaining the powerful
language understanding and generation capabilities of current systems.

3     Related Work
This section reviews related work across several research areas that inform the design of knowl-
edge agents. We organize the review by component and highlight how existing work ad-
dresses—or fails to address—the requirements for evidence-aware, long-horizon knowledge sys-
tems.

3.1   Retrieval-Augmented Generation
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems combine parametric memory in language models
with non-parametric memory through retrieval [2]. These systems retrieve relevant documents
from a knowledge base and condition language model generation on the retrieved context, en-
abling access to up-to-date information beyond the model’s training data.
    Early RAG systems [2] demonstrated the value of combining retrieval with generation, show-
ing improvements on knowledge-intensive tasks. Subsequent work has improved retrieval quality
through dense passage retrieval [8], hybrid search combining dense and sparse retrieval, and
learned retrieval strategies.
    However, current RAG systems exhibit several limitations relevant to knowledge agents:

    • Stateless operation: Most RAG systems are stateless, treating each query independently
      without maintaining context or knowledge across sessions.

    • Single-turn focus: Retrieval is typically performed once per query, without iterative
      refinement or evidence accumulation.

    • Implicit evidence: While RAG systems use retrieved documents, they do not explicitly
      track which evidence supports which claims, or evaluate evidence quality.

    • No self-evaluation: RAG systems do not assess whether retrieved information is suffi-
      cient or reliable before generating responses.

    Recent advances include self-RAG systems that reflect on retrieval quality, adaptive RAG
that dynamically adjusts retrieval strategies, and long-context RAG that handles extended con-
texts. However, these still operate primarily in single-turn, stateless contexts and do not address
long-horizon knowledge accumulation.

3.2   Memory-Augmented Language Models
A parallel line of research has explored augmenting language models with external memory
mechanisms. These approaches aim to extend the effective context window and enable models
to maintain information across longer sequences.
    Memorizing transformers and related architectures introduce external memory banks that
can store and retrieve information. Memory networks and episodic memory systems enable mod-
els to explicitly store and recall past experiences. Recent work has explored long-term memory
for language models, including methods for maintaining context across extended interactions.
    However, existing memory-augmented systems have limitations:

    • Context extension focus: Most work focuses on extending context windows rather than
      building persistent knowledge bases.

    • No evidence tracking: Memory systems store information but do not track evidence
      relationships or evaluate evidence quality.

    • Limited temporal awareness: Memory systems typically do not track how information
      evolves over time or handle temporal validity.

                                                8
                            From Chatbots to Knowledge Agents


   • No self-evaluation: Memory systems do not assess the reliability or adequacy of stored
     information.

    While memory-augmented systems address the statelessness limitation, they do not provide
the evidence awareness and self-evaluation capabilities required for knowledge agents.

3.3   Long-Horizon Reasoning
Research on long-horizon reasoning has explored methods for enabling language models to engage
in extended reasoning chains. Chain-of-thought prompting demonstrates that explicit reasoning
steps improve performance on complex tasks. Tree of thoughts and related approaches explore
tree-structured reasoning, considering multiple reasoning paths.
    ReAct and similar frameworks combine reasoning with action, enabling language models
to interact with external tools and environments. These approaches demonstrate the value of
multi-step reasoning and planning.
    However, long-horizon reasoning work has limitations:

   • Single-session focus: Most approaches operate within a single session without persistent
     memory across interactions.

   • No evidence tracking: Reasoning chains are generated but not explicitly linked to
     evidence sources or evaluated for evidence quality.

   • No knowledge accumulation: Each reasoning task starts from scratch rather than
     building on accumulated knowledge.

   • Limited self-evaluation: Systems generate reasoning chains but do not assess their own
     reliability or identify knowledge gaps.

3.4   Evidence-Based AI and Uncertainty Quantification
Recent work has recognized the importance of evidence tracking and uncertainty quantification
in AI systems. Some approaches link model outputs to training data or retrieved sources,
providing basic attribution. Uncertainty quantification methods estimate confidence in model
predictions, though often without explicit evidence tracking.
    Evidence-based reasoning systems explicitly connect claims to supporting evidence, enabling
traceability and verification. However, these approaches are typically:

   • Single-turn: Evidence is tracked within a single interaction but not across sessions.

   • Not integrated with memory: Evidence tracking is not combined with persistent mem-
     ory systems.

   • Limited evaluation: Evidence quality assessment is often basic or missing.

   • No temporal awareness: Evidence is not tracked over time or evaluated for temporal
     validity.

3.5   Agentic AI Frameworks
The rise of agentic AI has produced numerous frameworks for building AI agents, including
AutoGPT, LangChain, and others. These frameworks provide tools for building systems that
can use external tools, interact with environments, and perform multi-step tasks.
   However, current agent frameworks:

   • Task-focused: Emphasize task execution rather than knowledge accumulation.

   • Short-horizon: Typically operate over limited time horizons.

   • Limited memory: While some frameworks include memory, it is typically simple and
     not evidence-aware.

   • No evidence tracking: Frameworks do not emphasize evidence tracking or evaluation.

3.6   Trustworthy AI and Explainability
Research on trustworthy AI emphasizes transparency, reliability, and explainability. Explainable
AI methods aim to make model decisions interpretable, though often through post-hoc analysis
rather than inherent design.
    Evidence tracking provides a natural foundation for explainability, as claims can be traced
to their supporting evidence. However, existing explainability work:

   • Post-hoc: Often explains decisions after they are made rather than designing for explain-
     ability.

   • Not integrated: Explainability is not integrated with memory, long-horizon reasoning,
     or evidence evaluation.

   • Limited temporal dimension: Does not address how explanations should account for
     evolving knowledge over time.

The review above reveals several key gaps that knowledge agents address:

  4. Self-evaluation: Limited work on systems that assess their own reliability and identify

  5. Knowledge agents as a class: Knowledge agents have not been defined as a distinct

4     Knowledge Agents: Definition and Characteristics
In this section, we formalize the concept of knowledge agents and establish the distinguishing
characteristics that set them apart from traditional chatbots and other generative AI systems.

4.1     Definition
Definition 4.1 (Knowledge Agent). A Knowledge Agent is a generative AI system that:

    1. Maintains persistent memory across interactions, enabling knowledge accumulation and
       long-term reasoning

    2. Operates over extended temporal and cognitive horizons, supporting multi-step reasoning
       and iterative refinement

    3. Explicitly tracks and evaluates evidence supporting its claims and decisions

    4. Engages in self-evaluation to assess the adequacy and reliability of its knowledge and
       responses

    5. Treats answers as intermediate artifacts in an ongoing knowledge-building process rather
       than as final endpoints

    This definition emphasizes that knowledge agents are not simply chatbots with added fea-
tures, but represent a fundamentally different architectural paradigm optimized for sustained
knowledge work.

4.2     Core Characteristics
4.2.1    Persistent Memory
Knowledge agents maintain multiple forms of persistent memory [3, 4, 9]:

    • Episodic memory: Records of past interactions, decisions made, and their outcomes.
      This enables the agent to learn from experience and avoid repeating mistakes.

    • Semantic memory: Structured knowledge about facts, concepts, and relationships. This
      accumulates over time, building a knowledge base that grows with use.

    • Evidence memory: Explicit tracking of what information supports what claims, includ-
      ing source attribution, confidence levels, and temporal validity.




                                               11
                            From Chatbots to Knowledge Agents


4.2.2    Long-Horizon Reasoning
Unlike chat-based systems that optimize for immediate responses, knowledge agents are designed
for extended reasoning:
   • Goal decomposition: Complex goals are broken down into sequences of sub-goals, each
     of which may require multiple reasoning steps.
   • Iterative refinement: The agent can revisit and refine previous conclusions as new
     information becomes available.
   • Planning: The agent engages in explicit planning, considering multiple steps ahead rather
     than reacting only to the immediate context.

4.2.3    Evidence Awareness
Knowledge agents explicitly track and evaluate evidence:
   • Evidence collection: The agent actively seeks out information to support or refute
     claims, rather than relying solely on pre-existing knowledge.
   • Evidence evaluation: Mechanisms assess the quality, recency, and reliability of evidence,
     identifying gaps and contradictions.
   • Evidence traceability: Every claim can be traced back to its supporting evidence,
     enabling transparency and auditability.

4.2.4    Self-Evaluation
Knowledge agents include mechanisms for assessing their own reliability:
   • Confidence estimation: The agent quantifies its confidence in claims and decisions,
     distinguishing between high-confidence and uncertain conclusions.
   • Gap identification: The agent can identify when it lacks sufficient information to provide
     a reliable answer, and can explicitly communicate this uncertainty.
   • Reliability monitoring: The agent continuously monitors the grounding quality of its
     reasoning, flagging when evidence is weak or contradictory.

4.3     Distinguishing from Related Concepts
Knowledge agents differ from several related concepts:
   • vs. Chatbots: Chatbots are optimized for conversational fluency and immediate re-
     sponse. Knowledge agents prioritize evidence tracking, memory, and long-horizon reason-
     ing.
   • vs. RAG Systems: Retrieval-augmented generation systems enhance responses with
     retrieved information, but typically do so reactively and without persistent memory or
     evidence tracking.
   • vs. Autonomous Agents: While knowledge agents may include autonomous action
     capabilities, their primary focus is on knowledge work rather than task execution in the
     physical world.
   • vs. Expert Systems: Expert systems rely on hand-crafted knowledge bases and rules.
     Knowledge agents use learned representations and can adapt their knowledge through
     interaction.

4.4    Design Principles
Based on the characteristics above, we identify several key design principles for knowledge agents:

    1. Transparency over fluency: The system should prioritize making its reasoning and
       evidence explicit, even if this makes responses less fluent.

    2. Reliability over speed: The system should take time to gather adequate evidence rather
       than responding quickly with insufficient information.

    3. Iteration over finality: Answers are treated as working hypotheses that can be refined
       as more information becomes available.

    4. Traceability over abstraction: The system maintains explicit links between claims and
       evidence, rather than abstracting away these connections.

    These principles guide the architectural design presented in the following section.

   • Facts, concepts, and relationships extracted from interactions

   • What information supports what claims
   • Source attribution for each piece of evidence
   • Confidence levels and temporal validity

5.3.3    Control Loop
The control loop orchestrates the interaction between components and makes decisions about:

   • Whether to retrieve more information: If evidence coverage is insufficient, the loop
     triggers additional retrieval

   • Whether to abstain: If information is too uncertain or contradictory, the agent may
     explicitly communicate uncertainty rather than providing an unreliable answer

   • Whether to revise beliefs: When new evidence contradicts previous conclusions, the
     agent revises its understanding

   • When to respond: The agent responds only when it has sufficient confidence in its
     answer, or when it can clearly communicate its level of uncertainty

  2. Planning: The planner decomposes the goal into sub-goals

  3. Retrieval: The retriever gathers relevant information from knowledge sources and mem-
     ory

  4. Reasoning: The reasoning module processes the retrieved information and generates
     conclusions

  5. Evaluation: The evidence evaluator assesses whether sufficient evidence supports the
     conclusions

  6. Confidence assessment: The system estimates confidence and identifies knowledge gaps

  7. Decision: Based on confidence and evidence coverage, the system either:

         • Returns a response (if confidence is sufficient)
         • Retrieves additional information (if gaps are identified)
         • Communicates uncertainty (if information is insufficient)

  8. Memory update: Successful reasoning and evidence are stored in memory for future use

    This flow iterates until the system reaches sufficient confidence or determines that additional
information cannot be obtained.

6     Evidence Awareness Across Time
A defining characteristic of knowledge agents is their ability to maintain and evaluate evidence
over extended temporal horizons [7, 13]. This section explores how evidence awareness operates
across time and how it differs from the stateless evidence handling in traditional systems.

6.1    The Temporal Dimension of Evidence
In knowledge-intensive tasks, evidence is not static. Information changes over time, new sources
emerge, and the reliability of evidence may evolve. Knowledge agents must therefore:

    • Track evidence over time: Maintain records of what evidence was available at different
      points in time
    • Assess temporal validity: Determine whether evidence remains valid or has become
      outdated
    • Handle evolving information: Update conclusions when new evidence emerges or con-
      tradicts previous information
    • Maintain evidence provenance: Track where evidence came from and when it was
      obtained

6.2    Evidence Lifecycle
Evidence in a knowledge agent follows a lifecycle that extends beyond a single interaction:

    1. Collection: Evidence is gathered from various sources through retrieval
    2. Evaluation: Initial assessment of quality, relevance, and reliability
    3. Association: Evidence is linked to specific claims or conclusions
    4. Storage: Evidence is stored in memory with metadata (source, timestamp, confidence)
    5. Monitoring: Ongoing assessment of whether evidence remains valid
    6. Revision: Evidence may be updated, contradicted, or superseded by new information
    7. Retirement: Outdated or invalidated evidence is marked as such but may be retained
       for historical traceability

6.4     Evidence Coverage Assessment
A critical capability of knowledge agents is assessing whether they have sufficient evidence to
support a claim. This involves:

6.4.1    Coverage Metrics
   • Completeness: Are all aspects of the claim supported by evidence?

   • Depth: Is the evidence detailed enough to support the level of specificity in the claim?

   • Recency: Is the evidence current enough for the temporal context of the claim?

   • Authority: Do the evidence sources have sufficient credibility?

   • Consistency: Do multiple evidence sources agree, or are there conflicts?

6.4.2    Gap Identification
When evidence coverage is insufficient, the agent must identify specific gaps:


                                                 22
                             From Chatbots to Knowledge Agents


   • Missing information: What specific information is needed but not available?

   • Outdated information: What evidence exists but may be too old?

   • Conflicting information: Where do sources disagree, and what additional information
     might resolve conflicts?

   • Low-confidence areas: Where does evidence exist but with low reliability?

6.5   Evidence Contradiction Handling
A key challenge in long-horizon operation is handling contradictions between evidence sources.
The agent must:

  1. Detect contradictions: Identify when different sources make conflicting claims

  2. Assess source reliability: Evaluate which sources are more authoritative or recent

  3. Seek resolution: Actively look for additional information that might resolve the contra-
     diction

  4. Surface uncertainty: When contradictions cannot be resolved, explicitly communicate
     the uncertainty to the user

  5. Maintain traceability: Keep records of both conflicting claims and the reasoning about
     which to trust

6.6   Confidence Estimation
Knowledge agents must quantify their confidence in claims, recognizing that confidence can
change over time [11, 12]:

   • Evidence-based confidence: Confidence derived from the quality and quantity of sup-
     porting evidence

   • Source-based confidence: Confidence based on the reliability of evidence sources

   • Consistency-based confidence: Higher confidence when multiple independent sources
     agree

   • Temporal confidence: Confidence that decreases as evidence ages (for time-sensitive
     claims)

6.7   Abstention Mechanisms
A crucial capability that distinguishes knowledge agents from chatbots is the ability to abstain
from answering when information is insufficient. This requires:

   • Uncertainty thresholds: Clear criteria for when confidence is too low to provide a
     reliable answer

   • Gap communication: Explicitly communicating what information is missing

   • Partial answers: Providing answers for aspects where evidence is sufficient while ab-
     staining on others

   • Recommendations: Suggesting what additional information would enable a more com-
     plete answer

6.8       Evidence Traceability
For transparency and auditability, knowledge agents maintain explicit links between claims and
evidence:

    • Source attribution: Every claim can be traced to its supporting evidence sources

    • Reasoning chains: The logical steps from evidence to conclusion are preserved

    • Version history: Changes to conclusions as evidence evolves are tracked

    • Confidence evolution: How confidence in a claim has changed over time is recorded

    This traceability enables users to understand not just what the agent believes, but why it
believes it, and how that belief has evolved.

    6. Revised conclusion: With additional evidence, the agent revises its assessment and
       communicates the updated conclusion with higher confidence.

    7. Memory retention: All evidence, including the initial uncertain assessment and subse-
       quent revisions, is retained in memory for future reference.

   This example illustrates how evidence awareness operates across time, enabling the agent to
maintain accurate, up-to-date knowledge while preserving transparency about uncertainty and
evolution.

8.4.1    Step-by-Step Execution Flow
For each query, the knowledge agent executes the following exact sequence:

  1. Query Reception: User query is received and logged with timestamp.

  2. Episodic Memory Check: System checks episodic memory for similar past queries. If
     found, relevant past episodes are retrieved to inform current processing.

                          From Chatbots to Knowledge Agents


3. Initial Retrieval (Iteration 1):

     • Query is embedded using Sentence-Transformers (all-MiniLM-L6-v2)
     • Vector store is searched using cosine similarity (top-k=5)
     • Retrieved documents are converted to Evidence objects with metadata:
         – Evidence ID (unique identifier)
         – Source (arXiv ID or Wikipedia title)
         – Source type (arxiv/wikipedia)
         – Quality score (similarity score)
         – Timestamp

4. Evidence Evaluation:

     • Evidence coverage is assessed across 5 dimensions:
         – Completeness: Are all query aspects covered?
         – Depth: Is there sufficient detail?
         – Recency: Is evidence current?
         – Authority: Are sources credible?
         – Consistency: Do sources agree?
     • Overall coverage score is calculated (weighted average)
     • Knowledge gaps are identified using LLM-based gap analysis

5. Confidence Estimation:

     • Confidence = 0.4 × average_evidence_quality + 0.6 × coverage_score
     • Confidence threshold: 0.7 for high confidence
     • Coverage threshold: 0.8 for sufficient evidence

6. Decision Point:

     • If coverage > 0.8 AND confidence > 0.7: Proceed to response generation
     • Else: Trigger additional retrieval (Iteration 2)

7. Iterative Retrieval (if needed):

     • Query is refined based on identified gaps
     • Additional retrieval is performed with gap-specific terms
     • New evidence is evaluated and merged with existing evidence
     • Process repeats until thresholds are met or max iterations (3) reached

8. Response Generation:

     • LLM generates response with:
         – System prompt emphasizing evidence-based responses
         – Retrieved evidence (top 3 sources)
         – Past similar queries (if available)
         – Instructions to cite sources and indicate confidence
     • Response includes explicit source citations

9. Memory Update:

         • Evidence is stored in evidence memory
         • Claim is created linking response to evidence IDs
         • Episode is stored in episodic memory (query, response, evidence IDs)

8.4.2    Key Differences from Traditional RAG
Our Knowledge Agent differs from traditional RAG in several critical ways:

  1. Iterative Retrieval: Unlike RAG’s single-pass retrieval, our system performs multiple
     retrieval iterations based on evidence gaps, improving coverage by an average of 15% per
     iteration.

  2. Evidence Evaluation: RAG does not evaluate evidence quality or coverage. Our system
     explicitly assesses evidence across 5 dimensions before generating responses.

  3. Memory Systems: RAG is stateless. Our system maintains three types of memory:

         • Episodic memory for past interactions
         • Evidence memory for claim-evidence links
         • Semantic memory for accumulated knowledge

  4. Confidence Communication: RAG provides no uncertainty quantification. Our system
     estimates and communicates confidence scores (average 0.78 in real experiments).

  5. Explicit Evidence Tracking: RAG retrieves documents but doesn’t track evidence
     explicitly. Our system creates Evidence objects with full provenance and quality scores.

  6. Adaptive Retrieval: RAG uses fixed retrieval strategy. Our system adapts retrieval
     based on identified knowledge gaps and evidence quality.

    • Evidence Tracking: Unlike stateless chatbots, the system explicitly tracks evidence
      sources and maintains evidence memory.

    • Iterative Refinement: The system can refine responses through multiple retrieval iter-
      ations, unlike single-turn systems.

    • Confidence Communication: The system quantifies and communicates uncertainty,
      enabling users to assess response reliability.

    • Long-Horizon Operation: Memory systems enable knowledge accumulation across ses-
      sions, addressing the statelessness limitation.

8.10     Implications
These results demonstrate that:

    1. The knowledge agent architecture is implementable using local, open-source tools (Ollama,
       ChromaDB)

    2. Evidence-aware retrieval and evaluation function effectively in practice, providing reliable
       confidence estimates

    3. Iterative refinement improves evidence coverage, validating the control loop design

    4. The framework addresses key limitations of current chatbot systems while maintaining
       response quality

   These findings support the paper’s central thesis: that knowledge agents represent a viable
evolution from chatbots, with evidence-aware architectures enabling more reliable, transparent,
and long-horizon knowledge work.


9     Open Challenges
While the knowledge agent architecture addresses significant limitations of current systems, sev-
eral open challenges remain [14, 15]. This section identifies key research questions and technical
obstacles that must be addressed for knowledge agents to achieve their full potential.

9.1    Memory Scalability
As knowledge agents accumulate information over time, their memory systems must scale effi-
ciently. Challenges include:

    • Storage efficiency: How to store growing amounts of information without exponential
      growth in storage requirements

   • Retrieval efficiency: How to quickly retrieve relevant information from large memory
     stores

   • Memory consolidation: How to summarize and compress old information while pre-
     serving important details

   • Forgetting mechanisms: When and how to remove or archive outdated information
     without losing valuable historical context

9.2   Consistency Maintenance
Maintaining consistency in a growing knowledge base is challenging:

   • Contradiction resolution: How to automatically resolve conflicts between different
     pieces of information

   • Belief revision: How to update beliefs when new evidence contradicts previous conclu-
     sions

   • Version management: How to track how knowledge has evolved over time while main-
     taining current accurate state

   • Circular dependencies: How to handle situations where claims depend on each other
     in complex ways

9.3   Evidence Quality Assessment
Determining the quality and reliability of evidence is non-trivial:

   • Source credibility: How to automatically assess the credibility of information sources

   • Fact verification: How to verify factual claims, especially in domains where ground truth
     is difficult to establish

   • Bias detection: How to identify and account for biases in evidence sources

   • Propaganda and misinformation: How to detect and filter out intentionally misleading
     information

9.4   Uncertainty Quantification
Accurately quantifying uncertainty is essential but difficult:

   • Epistemic vs. aleatory uncertainty: Distinguishing between uncertainty due to lack
     of knowledge versus inherent randomness

   • Confidence calibration: Ensuring that confidence estimates accurately reflect true reli-
     ability

   • Uncertainty propagation: How uncertainty in evidence propagates through reasoning
     chains

   • Threshold determination: Setting appropriate thresholds for when to abstain from
     answering

9.5   Retrieval Strategy Optimization
Designing effective retrieval strategies is complex:

   • Gap identification: How to identify what information is missing when you don’t know
     what you don’t know

   • Query formulation: How to generate effective retrieval queries based on identified gaps

   • Source selection: How to choose which knowledge sources to query

   • Stopping criteria: When to stop retrieving additional information and proceed with
     available evidence

9.6   Reasoning Efficiency
Long-horizon reasoning can be computationally expensive:

   • Computational cost: Multi-step reasoning requires multiple LLM calls, increasing la-
     tency and cost

   • Reasoning depth: How to determine the appropriate depth of reasoning for a given task

   • Parallelization: How to parallelize reasoning steps when possible

   • Caching: How to cache and reuse reasoning results when appropriate

9.7   Evaluation and Benchmarking
Evaluating knowledge agents is challenging because traditional metrics may not capture their
key capabilities:

   • Long-horizon evaluation: How to evaluate performance on tasks that span multiple
     sessions

   • Evidence quality metrics: How to measure the quality of evidence tracking and evalu-
     ation

   • Reliability metrics: How to assess whether confidence estimates are well-calibrated

   • User trust: How to measure whether users trust and find value in knowledge agent
     responses

9.8   Privacy and Security
Knowledge agents raise privacy and security concerns:

   • Data privacy: How to protect sensitive information stored in memory

   • Source attribution: How to handle cases where source information is sensitive

   • Adversarial inputs: How to protect against malicious inputs designed to corrupt the
     knowledge base

   • Access control: How to manage who can access and modify different parts of the knowl-
     edge base

11      Conclusion
This paper has introduced knowledge agents as a new class of generative AI systems designed
for evidence-aware, long-horizon knowledge work. We have argued that current chat-based
systems, while impressive in their conversational capabilities, are fundamentally limited by their
statelessness, short-horizon focus, and answer-centric design. These limitations prevent them
from effectively supporting complex, knowledge-intensive tasks that require sustained reasoning,
memory, and reliability over time.

11.1    Key Contributions
Our primary contributions include:

  1. Formalization of knowledge agents: We have defined knowledge agents as a distinct
     system class, characterized by persistent memory, long-horizon reasoning, evidence aware-
     ness, and self-evaluation capabilities.

  2. Reference architecture: We have proposed an architectural framework that integrates
     retrieval, memory, reasoning, and evidence evaluation into a unified system designed for
     long-horizon operation.

     across temporal horizons, including evidence lifecycle, coverage assessment, contradiction
     handling, and traceability.

     iteration, and traceability over fluency and speed.

  5. Research agenda: We have identified open challenges and outlined future research di-
     rections that will advance the field.

11.2    Implications
The transition from chatbots to knowledge agents has several important implications:

   • For AI research: Knowledge agents represent a shift in focus from conversational fluency
     to epistemic reliability, opening new research questions about memory, evidence, and long-
     horizon reasoning.

   • For system design: The architecture emphasizes modularity, allowing different com-
     ponents (retrieval, memory, reasoning) to be improved independently while maintaining
     system coherence.

   • For evaluation: New metrics and benchmarks are needed that capture long-horizon
     reasoning, evidence quality, and reliability rather than just response quality.

   • For deployment: Knowledge agents may require different interaction patterns and user
     interfaces that make long-horizon reasoning transparent and understandable.

A.1.1    Retrieval Layer Implementation
The retrieval layer can be implemented using various approaches:

    • Dense retrieval: Vector embeddings (e.g., from sentence transformers) enable semantic
      similarity search

    • Sparse retrieval: Traditional keyword-based search (BM25, TF-IDF) for exact matches

    • Hybrid approaches: Combining dense and sparse retrieval for improved recall and pre-
      cision

    • Learned retrieval: Training retrieval models specifically for evidence gathering

A.1.2    Memory Implementation Strategies
Several strategies can be used for implementing persistent memory:

    • Vector databases: Storing embeddings in vector databases (e.g., Pinecone, Weaviate)
      for efficient similarity search

    • Graph databases: Using knowledge graphs to represent relationships between concepts

    • Relational databases: Structured storage for episodic memory and metadata

    • Hierarchical storage: Combining fast-access caches with slower archival storage

A.2     Example Workflow
This section provides a detailed example of how a knowledge agent processes a complex query.

A.2.1    Query
“Analyze the potential impacts of implementing a carbon tax policy, considering economic,
environmental, and social factors.”

A.2.2    Processing Steps
  1. Goal decomposition: The planner breaks this into sub-goals:
        • Understand carbon tax mechanisms
        • Analyze economic impacts
        • Analyze environmental impacts
        • Analyze social impacts
        • Synthesize findings
  2. Initial retrieval: The retriever gathers:
        • Academic papers on carbon taxes
        • Policy documents from jurisdictions with carbon taxes
        • Economic analyses
        • Environmental impact studies
  3. Reasoning: The reasoning module processes retrieved information and generates initial
     conclusions for each sub-goal.
  4. Evidence evaluation: The evaluator assesses:
        • Coverage: Sufficient information on economic and environmental impacts, but limited
          on social impacts
        • Quality: High-quality sources for economic analysis, mixed quality for social analysis
        • Recency: Most sources are recent (within 5 years)
  5. Confidence assessment:
        • Economic impacts: High confidence (strong evidence)
        • Environmental impacts: High confidence (strong evidence)
        • Social impacts: Medium confidence (limited evidence)
  6. Additional retrieval: The system identifies a gap in social impact information and
     retrieves additional sources.
  7. Revised reasoning: With additional evidence, the system refines its assessment of social
     impacts.
  8. Final response: The system provides a comprehensive analysis with:
        • Explicit citations to evidence sources
        • Confidence levels for each claim
        • Identification of remaining uncertainties
        • Traceable reasoning chains
  9. Memory update: All evidence, conclusions, and reasoning are stored in memory for
     future reference.

  • Knowledge Agent: A generative AI system with persistent memory, long-horizon rea-
